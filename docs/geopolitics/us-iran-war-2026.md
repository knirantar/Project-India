# US Iran War

## One-Line Summary
Since February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel have been in open armed conflict with Iran; Iran’s Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was killed in the opening strikes, and the U.S. is now enforcing a naval blockade of Iranian ports while a fragile, repeatedly extended ceasefire coexists with sporadic fire and a Strait of Hormuz crisis that has triggered the IEA-described “largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market,” with major implications for India’s energy security and maritime trade. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/8de8054f3abd4688f894c657467ee3dd?utm_source=openai))

## Why This Matters
- India imports ~88–89% of its crude oil; most India-bound energy cargoes normally transit the Strait of Hormuz. Disruptions since March 2026 have stranded Indian/India-bound ships and raised global energy prices. ([indianexpress.com](https://indianexpress.com/article/explained/explained-economics/indias-reliance-on-imported-oil-may-hit-fresh-high-in-fy26-10548802/?utm_source=openai))
- The U.S. blockade and Iran’s counter-measures have created a dual-restriction environment at Hormuz, directly affecting India’s energy inflows, insurance costs, and shipping risk. ([cfr.org](https://www.cfr.org/articles/united-states-blockades-strait-of-hormuz-after-u-s-iran-talks/?utm_source=openai))
- India has a 10‑year Chabahar port contract (signed May 13, 2024) but the U.S. revoked the 2018 Chabahar sanctions exception in September 2025, later granting a temporary carve‑out only until April 26, 2026—raising sanctions-exposure risk for Indian entities. ([pib.gov.in](https://pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2020454&utm_source=openai))

## Current State
Facts (as of May 10, 2026):
- Start and character of the war: U.S. and Israel launched major strikes on Iran on February 28, 2026 (Operation Epic Fury), and Iran responded with missile/drone salvos; Iran’s Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was killed that day. ([centcom.mil](https://www.centcom.mil/MEDIA/STATEMENTS/Statements-View/Article/4418396/us-forces-launch-operation-epic-fury/?utm_source=openai))
- Naval blockade: CENTCOM announced an impartial blockade of ships entering/exiting Iranian ports effective April 13, 2026; multiple Iranian-flagged tankers have been disabled enforcing the blockade. ([centcom.mil](https://www.centcom.mil/MEDIA/PRESS-RELEASES/Press-Release-View/Article/4457255/us-to-blockade-ships-entering-or-exiting-iranian-ports/))
- Strait of Hormuz: Iran intermittently closed or restricted Hormuz; the U.S. launched “Project Freedom” (from May 4) to “guide” merchant ships while maintaining the blockade; ceasefire claims coexist with exchanges of fire and mine-clearance operations. ([centcom.mil](https://www.centcom.mil/MEDIA/PRESS-RELEASES/Press-Release-View/Article/4476318/us-military-supports-launch-of-project-freedom-in-strait-of-hormuz/))
- Diplomacy: A conditional ceasefire was arranged on April 8 and extended, but Islamabad talks on April 11–12 failed; Council dynamics are split (UNSC Res. 2817 adopted Mar 11 condemning Iranian regional attacks; a later Hormuz resolution effort failed on Apr 7 due to vetoes). ([commonslibrary.parliament.uk](https://commonslibrary.parliament.uk/research-briefings/cbp-10637/))
- Nuclear file: IAEA Director General Grossi said on March 2 there was “no indication” nuclear facilities were hit in initial strikes; he urged strict verification in any end‑of‑war deal. ([investing.com](https://www.investing.com/news/world-news/no-sign-irans-nuclear-sites-were-hit-iaea-says-but-iran-alleges-one-was-4533937?utm_source=openai))
- India-specific: DG Shipping issued multiple advisories (from Feb 28 onward); MEA has advised Indians to leave Iran and avoid travel; Indian Navy is actively escorting vessels under Operation Sankalp; at least 22 India-bound ships and 611 seafarers were reported stranded west of Hormuz in mid‑March. ([dgshipping.gov.in](https://www.dgshipping.gov.in/WriteReadData/News/202602280342249592926MARSECDGSCircular08of2026dated28Feb20262.pdf?utm_source=openai))

Interpretation:
- The blockade/escort “dual regime” at Hormuz has moved risk from random interdiction to systematic state-on-state naval enforcement, compressing insurance capacity and deterring transits even when windows open. ([csis.org](https://www.csis.org/analysis/strait-hormuz-8-charts?utm_source=openai))

Provisional/volatile items:
- Ceasefire status and scope, numbers of successful transits during any “open” periods, and the terms of any draft end‑of‑war memo remain fluid; follow official communiqués and IMO/UKMTO notices. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/2749dc877f0ac34a0ccd4f0530786009?utm_source=openai))

## Historical Context
- 1979–2015: Cycles of confrontation, culminating in JCPOA (2015). 
- 2018–2024: U.S. exit from JCPOA; escalating proxy attacks; Jan 28, 2024 Tower 22 drone attack (Jordan) killed 3 U.S. soldiers; U.S. retaliatory strikes Feb 2, 2024. ([centcom.mil](https://www.centcom.mil/MEDIA/PRESS-RELEASES/Press-Release-View/Article/3658552/update-us-casualties-in-northeast-jordan-near-syrian-border/?utm_source=openai))
- 2024–2025: Iran‑Israel direct strikes (2024) and subsequent escalations set conditions for the 2026 war (as documented by parliamentary/think-tank timelines). ([commonslibrary.parliament.uk](https://commonslibrary.parliament.uk/research-briefings/cbp-10637/))
- Precedent: The 1980s “Tanker War” saw U.S. convoying and clashes (Earnest Will/Praying Mantis), but shipping did not largely halt as it has in 2026. ([congress.gov](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R45281?utm_source=openai))

## Data and Indicators
- Energy chokepoint baseline: ~21 mb/d of oil (≈20–21% of global liquids) and significant LNG volumes normally transit Hormuz. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=39932&os=avdavdxhup&utm_source=openai))
- Disruption magnitude: IEA characterizes the present shock as the “largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market.” ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/reports/sheltering-from-oil-shocks/introduction-and-context?utm_source=openai))
- Price signals: Brent touched ~$112/bbl in early April amid closures; volatility remains elevated. (Data consolidated from IEA/market summaries.) ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/reports/oil-market-report-march-2026?ftag=MSFd61514f&utm_source=openai))
- India’s oil import dependence: ~88–89% in FY25–FY26 (PPAC-cited reporting). ([indianexpress.com](https://indianexpress.com/article/explained/explained-economics/indias-reliance-on-imported-oil-may-hit-fresh-high-in-fy26-10548802/?utm_source=openai))
- India’s SPR: 5.33 MMT capacity; ~64% filled as of March 23, 2026—about 9.5 days of cover at 100% capacity. ([indianexpress.com](https://indianexpress.com/article/explained/explained-economics/india-strategic-petroleum-reserve-capacity-west-asia-10597131/?utm_source=openai))
- Indian shipping exposure: 22 India‑bound vessels and 611 seafarers stranded west of Hormuz reported mid‑March; Indian Navy escorts ongoing. ([ndtv.com](https://www.ndtv.com/india-news/how-indian-navy-ships-are-escorting-vessels-amid-strait-of-hormuz-closure-shivalik-nanda-devi-jag-laadki-iran-war-gulf-of-oman-aden-11226419?pfrom=home-ndtv_topscroll_Imagetopscroll))
- U.S. blockade enforcement actions: multiple Iranian-flagged tankers disabled or diverted (e.g., May 6 and May 8 incidents). ([centcom.mil](https://www.centcom.mil/MEDIA/PRESS-RELEASES/Press-Release-View/Article/4480820/us-disables-2-more-vessels-violating-blockade-in-gulf-of-oman/))

## Strengths
- Indian Navy presence and experience (Sankalp) in the Gulf enhances ability to shepherd India-bound cargoes during partial openings. ([pib.gov.in](https://www.pib.gov.in/Pressreleaseshare.aspx?PRID=1835382&utm_source=openai))
- Diversified crude slate post‑2022 (incl. Russia) offers some flexibility, though Hormuz shock dominates regional flows. ([dgciskol.gov.in](https://www.dgciskol.gov.in/writereaddata/Downloads/20251024153605Insights%20into%20Import%20of%20Crude%20Oil%20and%20International%20Crude%20Oil%20prices%20.pdf?utm_source=openai))
- Strategic clarity on Chabahar as an Indian anchor on Iran’s coast (contracted), potentially important post‑war. ([pib.gov.in](https://pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2020454&utm_source=openai))

## Bottlenecks
- High import dependence on crude and LNG; limited SPR cover compared with duration/intensity of disruption. ([indianexpress.com](https://indianexpress.com/article/explained/explained-economics/indias-reliance-on-imported-oil-may-hit-fresh-high-in-fy26-10548802/?utm_source=openai))
- Sanctions risk to Indian public/private entities operating near Iran (Chabahar/port services/insurance) after U.S. waiver revocation. ([dentons.com](https://www.dentons.com/en/insights/alerts/2025/september/17/maximum-pressure?utm_source=openai))
- Legal/operational uncertainty at Hormuz with overlapping ceasefire, blockade, and Iranian enforcement claims deters shippers and insurers. ([ungeneva.org](https://www.ungeneva.org/en/news-media/news/2026/04/117441/security-council-russia-and-china-veto-resolution-strait-hormuz?utm_source=openai))

## Global Comparison
- Unlike the 1980s Tanker War, the 2026 crisis produced a near-halt in transits for extended periods due to mines, drones, and insurance paralysis, magnifying macroeconomic impact. ([congress.gov](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R45281?utm_source=openai))

## Risks
- Prolonged blockade/closure dynamics at Hormuz could pressure India’s current account, rupee, and inflation; domestic price‑management measures have fiscal costs. ([business-standard.com](https://www.business-standard.com/economy/news/government-cuts-fuel-excise-duty-imposes-export-levy-on-diesel-atf-126032700327_1.html?utm_source=openai))
- Escalation channels (Lebanon/Hezbollah, Yemen/Red Sea) could expand maritime risk envelope for India‑flagged ships. ([commonslibrary.parliament.uk](https://commonslibrary.parliament.uk/research-briefings/cbp-10637/))
- Regulatory exposure if U.S. sanctions tighten or secondary sanctions extend to counterparties supporting India‑linked flows via Iran. ([dentons.com](https://www.dentons.com/en/insights/alerts/2025/september/17/maximum-pressure?utm_source=openai))

## Future Scenarios
- Negotiated endgame (most benign): A short “one‑page” framework—nuclear constraints, Hormuz reopening, phased sanctions relief—leads to demobilization and insurance normalization in Q3–Q4 2026. This remains uncertain. ([axios.com](https://www.axios.com/2026/05/06/iran-us-deal-one-page-memo?utm_source=openai))
- Prolonged standoff: Ceasefire holds on paper while U.S. blockade + Iranian counter‑pressure persist; sporadic violence, partial ship “windows,” elevated premiums; India continues expensive end‑around sourcing and naval escorts. ([cfr.org](https://www.cfr.org/articles/united-states-blockades-strait-of-hormuz-after-u-s-iran-talks/?utm_source=openai))
- Renewed escalation: Talks fail, large strikes resume (energy infrastructure/bridges threatened), prompting broader regional spillover and deeper oil/gas supply shock. ([commonslibrary.parliament.uk](https://commonslibrary.parliament.uk/research-briefings/cbp-10637/))
