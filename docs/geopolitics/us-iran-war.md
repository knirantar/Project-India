# US Iran War

## One-Line Summary
As of May 10, 2026, a fragile April ceasefire in the 2026 U.S.–Iran war has been repeatedly tested by fresh exchanges around the Strait of Hormuz, severely disrupting Gulf energy flows and creating immediate energy-security, macroeconomic, and maritime-risk challenges for India. ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/reports/oil-market-report-april-2026))

## Why This Matters
- India’s energy security is directly exposed to Hormuz: about 20.9 mb/d of oil transited the strait in 1H 2025 (roughly 20% of global liquids), and almost all LNG from Qatar/UAE crosses Hormuz; Asian importers, including India, are the main destinations. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/international/content/analysis/special_topics/World_Oil_Transit_Chokepoints/))
- India’s oil import dependence rose to ~88% in FY25; supply shocks quickly pass through to inflation, the current account, and the rupee. ([indianexpress.com](https://indianexpress.com/article/business/india-oil-natural-gas-reliance-energy-demand-domestic-production-sluggish-9954570/lite/?utm_source=openai))
- Roughly “one crore” (≈10 million) Indian citizens work in the Gulf; conflict and shipping attacks threaten their safety and remittances. ([pib.gov.in](https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2235161))
- War-risk insurance and convoy dynamics around Hormuz have sharply raised maritime costs and uncertainty for Indian trade. ([theguardian.com](https://www.theguardian.com/business/2026/mar/11/lloyds-of-london-insurance-shipping-strait-of-hormuz-us-israel-iran?utm_source=openai))

## Current State
- Ceasefire status: A two-week ceasefire announced in early April (April 8 local) eased but did not end hostilities; on May 7 U.S. destroyers and Iranian forces exchanged fire in the Strait of Hormuz, and the U.S. conducted “self-defense” strikes on Iranian targets. Negotiations on a one-page understanding to end the war continue. ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/reports/oil-market-report-april-2026))
- Shipping and escorts: CENTCOM launched “Project Freedom” (May 3) to help reopen Hormuz and protect transits; risk of escalation remains. ([centcom.mil](https://www.centcom.mil/MEDIA/PRESS-RELEASES/Press-Release-View/Article/4476318/us-military-supports-launch-of-project-freedom-in-strait-of-hormuz/?utm_source=openai))
- India’s position and stock cover: MEA has been engaging Tehran on safe passage; as of May 7, MEA said 11 Indian ships exited Hormuz and 13 remained in the Gulf. The Petroleum Ministry states India’s total liquid-fuels stock cover is about 60 days at present (capacity ~74 days) and supplies are “secure and under control.” ([government.economictimes.indiatimes.com](https://government.economictimes.indiatimes.com/amp/news/secure-india/11-indian-ships-exited-strait-of-hormuz-13-still-in-gulf-says-mea/130900252?utm_source=openai))

## Historical Context
- After U.S. sanctions on Iran’s oil from 2018, India’s Iranian crude imports fell to zero by mid‑2019; the Indian Navy began Operation Sankalp (June 19, 2019) to protect Indian-flag shipping in the Gulf. ([pib.gov.in](https://www.pib.gov.in/Pressreleaseshare.aspx?PRID=1835382))
- India signed a 10‑year contract (May 13, 2024) to equip and operate Chabahar’s Shahid Beheshti terminal; the U.S. noted potential sanctions risk. ([pib.gov.in](https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2020454&utm_source=openai))
- The 2026 war commenced on February 28, 2026, per India’s official statement; a ceasefire was announced in early April. ([pib.gov.in](https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2235161))

## Relevant Policies, Laws, and Programs
- India: Operation Sankalp (maritime security escorts); Strategic Petroleum Reserves (ISPRL: 5.33 MMT; Phase‑II 6.5 MMT approved); fuel taxation and price-policy levers; long-term LNG SPA with QatarEnergy (7.5 MTPA, 2028–2048). ([pib.gov.in](https://www.pib.gov.in/Pressreleaseshare.aspx?PRID=1835382))
- U.S.: Iran sanctions regimes (risk overlays for Chabahar and payments). ([ndtv.com](https://www.ndtv.com/world-news/chabahar-port-us-warns-of-potential-risk-of-sanctions-after-india-iran-sign-port-deal-5658155?utm_source=openai))
- Energy-security frameworks: IEA emergency stock mechanisms; EIA/IEA chokepoint monitoring. ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/data-and-statistics/data-tools/oil-stocks-of-iea-countries?utm_source=openai))

## Data and Indicators
- Hormuz oil transit (1H 2025): 20.9 mb/d total; crude/condensate 14.7 mb/d; products 6.1 mb/d; LNG 11.4 Bcf/d. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/international/content/analysis/special_topics/World_Oil_Transit_Chokepoints/))
- India oil import dependence: ~88.2% in FY25 (up from FY24). ([indianexpress.com](https://indianexpress.com/article/business/india-oil-natural-gas-reliance-energy-demand-domestic-production-sluggish-9954570/lite/?utm_source=openai))
- ISPRL capacity and fill: 5.33 MMT (≈9.5 days at full); “about two‑thirds full” as of Mar 2026 (GoI to Parliament). ([pib.gov.in](https://www.pib.gov.in/Pressreleaseshare.aspx?PRID=2113233&utm_source=openai))
- Brent/Indian basket price shock: Brent averaged ~$98/bbl in March 2026 (+41% m/m). ([fipi.org.in](https://www.fipi.org.in/Policy_Economic_Report/Policy%20Economic%20Report%20-%20March%202026.pdf?utm_source=openai))
- Price-pass-through: A 10% crude increase can raise India’s headline CPI by ~20–30 bps; $10/bbl for a year can worsen CAD by ≈0.4% of GDP. ([rbi.org.in](https://www.rbi.org.in/Scripts/BS_ViewBulletin.aspx?Id=23516&utm_source=openai))
- LNG exposure: ~93% of Qatar and 96% of UAE LNG use Hormuz; Bangladesh–India–Pakistan obtained almost two‑thirds of their LNG via Hormuz in 2025. ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/about/oil-security-and-emergency-response/strait-of-hormuz))
- MEA shipping status: 11 Indian ships exited Hormuz; 13 remained (May 7). ([government.economictimes.indiatimes.com](https://government.economictimes.indiatimes.com/amp/news/secure-india/11-indian-ships-exited-strait-of-hormuz-13-still-in-gulf-says-mea/130900252?utm_source=openai))

## Strengths
- Large refining system and diversified crude slate; ability to redirect to non‑Hormuz barrels (Russia, West Africa, U.S.). ([business-standard.com](https://www.business-standard.com/markets/commodities/opec-s-share-in-india-s-annual-oil-imports-rises-after-8-year-drop-125011700552_1.html?utm_source=openai))
- Strategic and commercial stocks jointly provide material cover (capacity ~74 days). ([pib.gov.in](https://www.pib.gov.in/Pressreleaseshare.aspx?PRID=1694712&utm_source=openai))
- Long-term LNG anchor with Qatar (7.5 MTPA) provides baseline volumes once flows normalize. ([petronetlng.in](https://www.petronetlng.in/w/petronet-lng-limited-executes-long-term-contract-for-purchase-of-7.5-mmtpa-lng-with-qatarenergy-1?utm_source=openai))

## Bottlenecks
- LNG dependence on Qatar via Hormuz exposes city‑gas/fertilizer sectors to curtailment risk. ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/about/oil-security-and-emergency-response/strait-of-hormuz))
- War-risk insurance costs (1–1.5% of hull value, up from ~0.25%) materially raise delivered crude/LPG costs. ([theguardian.com](https://www.theguardian.com/business/2026/mar/11/lloyds-of-london-insurance-shipping-strait-of-hormuz-us-israel-iran?utm_source=openai))
- SPR size is modest relative to import needs; Phase‑II not yet operational. ([pib.gov.in](https://www.pib.gov.in/Pressreleaseshare.aspx?PRID=2113233&utm_source=openai))

## Global Comparison
- Chokepoint scale: In 1H 2025, Malacca handled 23.2 mb/d vs 20.9 mb/d through Hormuz; both are vital to Asian importers (including India). ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/international/content/analysis/special_topics/World_Oil_Transit_Chokepoints/))
- Stockholding norms: IEA members hold ≥90 days of net imports; India (IEA association) has ~74 days total capacity. ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/data-and-statistics/data-tools/oil-stocks-of-iea-countries?utm_source=openai))

## Opportunities
- Accelerate SPR Phase‑II and consider product stockholding in LPG/diesel to cushion Hormuz-linked LNG and middle‑distillate shocks. ([pib.gov.in](https://www.pib.gov.in/Pressreleaseshare.aspx?PRID=2113233&utm_source=openai))
- Expand non‑Hormuz crude sourcing (e.g., West Africa, U.S. Gulf) and term deals with pipeline‑bypass Gulf exporters (Saudi East–West, UAE ADCOP to Fujairah). ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/about/oil-security-and-emergency-response/strait-of-hormuz))
- Use Chabahar for non‑energy trade resilience to Central Asia while managing sanctions risk. ([pib.gov.in](https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2020454&utm_source=openai))

## Risks
- Prolonged or renewed blockade of Hormuz would keep crude near triple‑digit levels and tighten products, raising India’s CPI/CAD and pressuring the rupee. ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/reports/oil-market-report-april-2026))
- Maritime incidents, mines, or miscalculation near Indian‑flag vessels could trigger evacuation/escort demands and insurance spikes. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/fdc6d2ae9396377919c967746fa9996b?utm_source=openai))
- Secondary sanctions exposure (payments, shipping, Chabahar) could complicate procurement/logistics. ([ndtv.com](https://www.ndtv.com/world-news/chabahar-port-us-warns-of-potential-risk-of-sanctions-after-india-iran-sign-port-deal-5658155?utm_source=openai))

## Future Scenarios
- Rapid de‑escalation: Ceasefire hardens; “Project Freedom” transits normalize; oil prices retrace; India leans on diversified sourcing and refills SPR. ([centcom.mil](https://www.centcom.mil/MEDIA/PRESS-RELEASES/Press-Release-View/Article/4476318/us-military-supports-launch-of-project-freedom-in-strait-of-hormuz/?utm_source=openai))
- Partial reopening/protracted tension: Limited volumes via Saudi/UAE bypass lines and convoys; high insurance; intermittent shortages in LPG/LNG; targeted Indian policy buffers invoked. ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/about/oil-security-and-emergency-response/strait-of-hormuz))
- Escalation/closure: Hormuz remains largely shut; IEA/coordinated stock releases continue; significant demand destruction and rationing pressures in Asia; India activates deeper fiscal/monetary and sectoral controls. ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/reports/oil-market-report-april-2026))

## Open Questions
- How quickly can escorted convoys and pipeline bypasses restore a meaningful share of pre‑war Hormuz flows? (Data gap: official throughput and convoy schedules by month.)
- What final ceasefire enforcement/verification will protect shipping? (Need: text of any U.S.–Iran understanding.)
- How much LNG shortfall would India face under a 30–60 day Hormuz disruption, and what are the formal prioritization rules by sector? (Need: MoPNG/DGH/PPAC granular LNG allocation data.)
- To what extent will India use fiscal levers (excise/VAT) versus OMC balance sheets to cushion retail prices if Brent remains >$100 for multiple months? (Need: MoF/MoPNG guidance.)
